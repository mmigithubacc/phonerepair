Cellphone Repair Course · Module 3 of 10

Disassembly & Reassembly

Open any phone. Close it again. Have it still work.

Day 2 of 5  ·  morning block  ·  ~1 h theory  ·  bench practical  ·  quiz

Safety recital first (2 random students). Then set the stakes: "Today you take a complete working teardown from closed phone to bare frame and back. Every repair in this course lives inside today's skill. Damage-free is the grade."

What you'll be able to do after today

  • Open a glued or clipped phone without damage
  • Disconnect every connector type the right way
  • Keep a screw map and photo record that lets anyone reassemble
  • Reassemble, test, and seal — in that order

Point out the ratio: short theory, long bench time. "The slides are short because the bench is long." Everything today is technique — the theory is just naming the traps before they walk into them.

The golden habit

Look up the model-specific guide BEFORE you open anything.

  • Where the cables live · where the battery sits · which screws differ
  • Every model hides its own traps — the guide already found them
  • Module 2 taught you to ID the model; now it becomes a graded reflex

Show two phones that look identical from outside but open in opposite directions (e.g. screen-first vs back-first). "Guess wrong and you pry the wrong panel, crack it, and now you owe the customer a part." From today, opening a device without consulting the guide loses rubric points automatically.

The universal opening sequence

  1. Power off — completely, not sleep
  2. Heat the seam to 80–100 °C to soften adhesive
  3. Suction cup lifts a first gap
  4. Pick rides the gap around the perimeter — shallow!

Before the pick goes in: know from the guide where the cables live. A deep pick in the wrong corner slices a flex you can't see.

Demo the full sequence on the doc camera. Show pick depth with tape as a depth stop: 2–3 mm max on most phones. Ask: "Why power off first?" — live boards short when tools bridge contacts, and a booted phone can fire the backlight circuit mid-pry. Heat check from Module 1: hot to touch, not scorching.

Adhesive phones vs clip phones

Adhesive (most modern)

Glued seams — need heat, suction, picks, patience. Water-resistant models glue hardest. Re-sealing needs new adhesive.

Clips (older / budget)

Plastic clips snap apart with a pick — no heat. But clips break if you rush; work the pick along, don't lever one point.

The guide tells you which you're facing — and which panel opens first.

Let students hear the difference: run a pick around a clip phone (clack-clack-clack) vs the silent drag of adhesive. Common beginner error on clip phones: skipping a hidden screw and snapping the clips around it. Rule: if a panel resists after clips release, hunt for the hidden screw — often under a label or foot.

Screw mapping on the magnetic mat

  • Draw the phone's zones on the mat; every screw lands in its own zone
  • Label lengths where they differ (e.g. "4.2 mm — shield")
  • Mapped mat = anyone can reassemble; mystery pile = nobody can

Long-screw damage kills boards. A screw 0.5 mm too long drills through copper layers under the hole. The phone dies instantly or weeks later — and it's unrepairable. The map is the only protection.

Show a real screw-map layout on the doc camera. Tell the iPhone 6 story: thousands of boards killed by techs swapping two near-identical screws near the display connector — it became famous enough to have a name. From now on, an unmapped bench is an automatic rubric deduction, exactly as promised in Module 1.

THE rule of internal work

Battery connector FIRST. Always. Every time.

The moment the phone is open, disconnect the battery before touching anything else. A live board + a metal tool = shorted backlight, fried charging IC, or worse. Reconnect it last.

This is the single most important slide today — slow down. Repeat the Module 1 story: screen swap with battery connected, backlight circuit dead on reconnect. Have the whole class say it aloud once: "Battery first." Corny, but it sticks. In the practical, any tool touching the board before battery disconnect = checkpoint failed, restart the stage.

Press-fit connectors: straight up, never sideways

  • Plastic spudger tip under the connector's edge — the correct side per the guide
  • Pry straight up — it pops like a tiny LEGO
  • Never wiggle sideways — that bends the pins and ruins socket and cable
  • Reconnect: align, then press until you feel the click

Doc-camera demo, several times, slow. Show a sacrificial connector with bent pins from sideways wiggling — under magnification the damage is obvious. Reconnection warning for later: if it doesn't click with light pressure, it's misaligned; pressing harder crushes pins. Lift, realign, retry.

ZIF connectors: latch first, then slide

  1. Find the hinged latch (usually a dark bar on the socket)
  2. Flip it up gently with a spudger tip or fingernail
  3. The cable now slides out freely — zero force

Never pull the cable against a closed latch — the flex tears before the latch gives. If a cable won't slide, the latch isn't open.

Demo open and close. The reinsertion detail everyone misses: slide the flex fully and squarely in BEFORE closing the latch — a crooked or half-inserted flex makes the part flaky or dead. "Zero force" is the self-check: if any step needs strength, you're doing it wrong.

Coax antenna cables: tiny, springy, airborne

  • Thin round black/white cables with a snap-on button end
  • Disconnect: spudger tip under the button, pop straight up
  • They're tiny and they love to fly — pop them low and slow, over the mat
  • Reconnect: position the button on the socket, press till it snaps

Pop one on camera — and warn that at real speed they ping across the room. Habit to teach: cup your other hand over the cable as it releases. Connect back to Module 2: an unclipped coax = "no signal" fault. On reassembly, a coax that looks seated but didn't snap will fall off at first bump.

Flex cable handling

  • No pulling — flexes are unplugged at connectors, never dragged out
  • No creasing — never fold sharper than the factory fold
  • Peel glued-down flexes slowly with a plastic tool, heat if stubborn

Tears are invisible until nothing works. A flex can look perfect and be dead inside. If you creased one, tell the trainer — in a shop, you'd tell the customer and replace it.

Callback to the Module 2 crease demo. Add the professional-ethics angle: hiding a damaged flex means the phone comes back "faulty" and the customer blames your parts. Owning mistakes at the bench is a graded behavior in this course — a reported mistake costs a point; a hidden one fails the checkpoint.

EMI shields: clipped vs soldered

Clipped

Snaps onto a frame of clips. Pops off with a spudger at the corners. Snaps back on.

Soldered

A solid soldered wall. Does not come off with pry tools — ever. Stays put until Module 7 (hot air).

Not sure which? Look at the edge: clips show tiny teeth, solder shows a smooth silver seam.

Show both edges under magnification. Beginners who pry a soldered shield rip pads off the board — an instant board-killer that looks like it should have worked. Rule for the practical: any shield that resists a gentle corner pry gets left alone and flagged to the trainer.

Teardown order — and why order matters

  1. Power off → open → battery disconnect
  2. Shields & brackets off (clipped only)
  3. Connectors off the board — screen, cameras, coax, port flexes
  4. Motherboard out → cameras, speakers, vibration motor
  5. Battery out last (adhesive pull-tabs, no prying) → bare frame

Order = safety: battery-first cuts power; brackets before connectors so nothing is pried against; board out before parts that hide under it. Wrong order = forced parts.

Walk the order on the projected ifixit guide for today's donor model. Emphasize the reason column, not memorization — orders differ per model, but the logic (de-energize → unblock → disconnect → extract) is universal. Battery pull-tabs: pull slow and low, parallel to the battery; if a tab snaps, IPA under the edge and patience — never metal.

Photograph every stage

  • One photo before each layer comes off — cables, screws, bracket positions
  • Your photos are your reassembly manual — better than memory, better than the guide (it shows your phone)
  • In a shop: photos are also your evidence of pre-existing damage

Costs 10 seconds a stage. Saves the "where did this bracket go?" hour.

Show a real stage-photo series from one of your own teardowns. The evidence point matters commercially: photograph corrosion, bend, or prior bodge-work BEFORE you touch it, or the customer will swear you caused it. Stage photos are a graded deliverable in today's practical.

Reassembly = reverse + test

  1. Rebuild in reverse order, photos as your checklist
  2. Every connector clicks; every screw back to its mapped hole
  3. Battery reconnected last
  4. TEST before final sealing: boot, touch, cameras, speakers, mic, signal, charge

Closing an untested phone means opening it twice. Adhesive is single-use — test first, seal once.

Teach the loose-assembly test: everything connected, screen resting in place unsealed, power on, run the checks. Only after all-pass do adhesive and final closure happen. Ask: "What does it cost to skip the test?" — a new adhesive kit, 30 minutes, and on a customer phone, your credibility.

The classic beginner mistakes

  • Torn flex — pulled instead of unlatched, or a deep pick
  • Flying coax — popped fast, lost forever under a bench
  • Forgotten screw — rattles, or presses into the battery
  • Forced connector at an angle — bent pins, dead part
  • Pry too deep — sliced cable or nicked battery
Common beginner mistakes — watch them happen — Video 3.3 in resources/videos.md

Before playing the video, have the class predict which mistake they think they'll make — writing it down primes them to avoid it. After the video, map each mistake back to its prevention slide (latch-first, cup your hand, screw map, click-check, shallow picks). Every one of these is preventable by technique, not talent.

Teach-back time

  1. One volunteer: talk the class through the universal opening sequence — including where picks must not go
  2. Another volunteer: demo all three connector types on the donor board — press-fit, ZIF, coax

Class: listen for anything missed — you're the reviewers.

The connector demo is the one that matters — put it under the doc camera and let the volunteer narrate. If they wiggle a press-fit sideways or pull a flex, freeze the moment and let the class name the error. Fix gaps yourself before releasing anyone to the benches.
