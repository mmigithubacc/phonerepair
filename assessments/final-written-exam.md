## Section B — Scenario short answers (5 × 8 pts = 40 pts)

Answer as the technician on duty. Bullet points welcome; marks are for correct, ordered, safe actions and honest communication.

**B1. (8 pts) No-power phone.**
A customer brings an Android phone: "It just died yesterday. Nothing happens when I press power." Describe your diagnosis plan, in order, from the counter to (if needed) opening the phone. Include the tools/readings you'd use and how you'd separate no-power from no-display and from a dead battery.

**B2. (8 pts) Water-damaged phone.**
A customer arrives with a phone that fell in a toilet 2 hours ago. It's still on, screen flickering. Write (i) what you say and do in the first two minutes, (ii) your treatment plan, step by step, and (iii) the prognosis/warranty conversation, including what you will replace as a matter of policy and why "rice" would have been the wrong advice.

**B3. (8 pts) Swollen battery mid-repair.**
Halfway through a screen replacement you lift the display and discover the battery is visibly swollen. The customer only paid for a screen. What do you do — immediately at the bench, and then with the customer? Include the safety handling, storage, disposal, and how you handle the job/pricing conversation.

**B4. (8 pts) Activation lock on a "found" phone.**
A walk-in customer presents an iPhone showing Activation Lock: "I found it, can you remove the lock? I'll pay double." Write your response and reasoning: what you will and won't do, what you tell the customer, what legitimate options exist (for a true owner), and why the shop policy is what it is.

**B5. (8 pts) Pricing a screen repair.**
A customer needs a screen for a mid-range Android phone. Your supplier offers: aftermarket panel $22, refurbished original $38, pulled original $55. Your labor rate values this 1-hour job at $25, and shop policy is a ~15% margin on the total. (i) Calculate a quote for each tier (show your arithmetic; sensible rounding is fine). (ii) Write, in 3–5 sentences, how you'd present the three options to the customer, including quality and warranty differences and your recommendation.

## Section B model answers & grading points (8 pts each)

Award the listed points; partial credit within each bullet at examiner discretion. An answer that includes a **dangerous action** (e.g. charging a wet phone, prying a battery with metal, offering a lock bypass) caps that question at 2/8.

**B1 — No-power diagnosis plan (8 pts)**
- (1 pt) Intake: ask history (drop? liquid? died gradually or suddenly?), photograph condition, note pre-existing damage.
- (2 pts) Rule out trivial causes first: known-good charger + cable, inspect/clean port for lint, leave on charge 15–30 min, try forced restart (button combo).
- (1 pt) USB ammeter reading: 0 A vs small draw vs normal draw — locates the fault along charger → cable → port → charging IC → PMIC → battery.
- (1 pt) Distinguish no-display: flashlight test for faint image, listen/feel for vibration, sounds, notification LED — if alive, it's a display fault, not power.
- (2 pts) If still dead: open the phone (ESD setup, battery disconnect first), inspect for damage/liquid (check LCIs), measure battery voltage (~3.0–4.4 V expected; well below 3.0 V = over-discharged/dead battery), substitution-test with a known-good battery.
- (1 pt) Escalation/honesty: if battery and port check out, suspect board (charging IC/PMIC) → quote board-level work or refer to micro-soldering specialist; report findings to customer before further cost.

**B2 — Water damage: conversation + treatment (8 pts)**
- (2 pts) First two minutes: power it OFF immediately, do NOT charge or keep testing; explain that every powered minute causes shorts and drives electrolytic corrosion; take intake details + photos; note toilet water is dirtier/more conductive than fresh water (and salt water would be worst).
- (3 pts) Treatment plan in order: full teardown; disconnect battery first; check LCI stickers to document exposure; remove shields where accessible; brush-clean corrosion with ≥99% IPA; IPA bath and/or ultrasonic clean the bare logic board (never the battery or assembled phone); dry thoroughly; reassemble with a NEW battery; test all functions before sealing.
- (1 pt) Rice myth: rice doesn't remove internal moisture or treat corrosion — it wastes the critical treatment window while corrosion spreads.
- (2 pts) Prognosis/warranty: honest odds; even a working phone may fail weeks/months later as corrosion continues; limited or no warranty on liquid-damaged boards; battery replaced as policy because wetted cells are a delayed fire/failure risk; urge immediate data backup.

**B3 — Swollen battery mid-repair (8 pts)**
- (2 pts) Bench safety: stop work immediately; do not press, bend, puncture, pry with metal, or charge it; keep heat sources away.
- (2 pts) If it must come out: only per safe procedure (plastic tools, adhesive released with IPA/patience, no bending); into the sand-filled battery bin / fire-safe container; trainer/senior handles per shop policy; never stored in the phone, never resold; dispose via battery recycling.
- (2 pts) Customer call BEFORE proceeding: phone the customer, explain the discovery (photos help), that a swollen battery is a safety hazard and cannot legally/ethically be refitted, and quote the added battery cost; get consent before doing paid extra work.
- (1 pt) If the customer refuses: explain you cannot reinstall a swollen battery; document the refusal; return the phone unassembled/safe or with battery quarantined per policy — never rebuild a hazard.
- (1 pt) Paperwork: record on the job sheet/intake form and warranty log; this protects the shop and sets the precedent of honest handling.

**B4 — Activation lock request (8 pts)**
- (2 pts) Clear refusal: the shop never bypasses Activation Lock or FRP — no exceptions, no price makes it OK.
- (2 pts) Reasoning: these are anti-theft protections; a "found" phone is someone's lost or stolen property; bypassing supports phone theft and is illegal/against platform terms; the offered double payment is itself a red flag.
- (2 pts) Legitimate paths (for a true owner): the owner signs in with their own Apple ID/Google account or removes the device from their account remotely; proof of purchase through official Apple/Google support channels; a genuinely found phone should go to the police/carrier so the owner can recover it (the lock's owner-contact screen may show a number).
- (1 pt) Optional professional step: check the IMEI (\*#06#) against lost/stolen databases; document the interaction.
- (1 pt) Customer handling: polite, firm, no lecture beyond policy; offer legitimate services instead (e.g. if they later prove ownership).
- *Auto-cap:* any answer offering a bypass, "unlock box," or referral to a bypass service scores max 2/8.

**B5 — Pricing + tier conversation (8 pts)**
- (4 pts) Arithmetic (1 pt per tier + 1 pt for consistent method). Accept parts+labor then +15% margin, sensibly rounded:
  - Aftermarket: (22 + 25) × 1.15 = **$54.05 → ~$54–55**
  - Refurbished original: (38 + 25) × 1.15 = **$72.45 → ~$72–75**
  - Pulled original: (55 + 25) × 1.15 = **$92.00 → ~$92–95**
  - Also acceptable: margin applied to parts only, or a flat markup — award full marks if the method is stated and consistently applied and labor + margin are both included.
- (3 pts) Customer presentation covers: what each tier IS (aftermarket = third-party panel, budget colors/brightness/touch may differ; refurbished = original glass/panel rebuilt; pulled = original from a donor, closest to factory); warranty scaled to tier (e.g. 3 months aftermarket, 6 months refurb, 12 months pulled — any sensible scaling accepted); an honest recommendation matched to the customer's budget and how long they'll keep the phone.
- (1 pt) Professional touches: no fake "OEM" claims, price quoted before work, tier + warranty written on the job sheet/receipt.
