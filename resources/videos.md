# Curated Video List — Cellphone Repair Technician Training

This is the master list of the videos referenced by number (▶ Video 1.1, 1.2 … 10.3) in the
lesson plans and slides. **Trainers: preview every video before class** — check it still exists,
matches the slot description, and is appropriate for your students' level. Where licensing and
local law permit, download an offline copy so a dead venue Wi-Fi connection doesn't sink the
lesson. YouTube links rot: videos get deleted, renamed, or region-blocked. Every entry therefore
carries a **fallback search phrase** — paste it into YouTube search and the same video (or a
close equivalent from the same channel) should be the top result. Entries marked **(search)**
mean we could not confirm a durable URL at curation time: use the search phrase to pick a good
current video from the named channel, then write your choice into this file. Entries whose
channel is marked *(unverified)* have a confirmed title and link but the uploading channel could
not be confirmed — verify it when you preview, and replace if the source looks low quality.

## Recommended channels to explore further

- **iFixit** (youtube.com/@iFixitYourself) — teardowns, repair guides, and repairability advocacy from the people behind the written-guide standard.
- **Hugh Jeffreys** (youtube.com/@HughJeffreys) — honest phone restorations and part-quality experiments; excellent realistic workflow footage.

**Written reference standard:** for any specific phone model, the iFixit written guides at
**ifixit.com** (Guides → your device) are the definitive step-by-step reference — photographed,
peer-reviewed, and versioned. Teach students to look up the iFixit guide as the first step of
every unfamiliar repair; videos supplement, the guide is the checklist.

## Building your own demo video library

The most reliable video source is the one you film yourself. Set a phone or webcam on an overhead
mount above the demo bench and record your own runs of each practical: your exact practice-phone
models, your tools, your adhesive, your sequence. Advantages:

- **No link rot** — files live on the classroom laptop and a backup drive, no Wi-Fi needed.
- **Perfect match** — students see the same phone model and bench layout they'll use minutes later.
- **Reusable everywhere** — pause/rewind during labs, share with absent students, reuse across cohorts.
- **Improves your teaching** — reviewing your own footage catches habits you don't narrate.

Start with the highest-value five: glued screen replacement, frame-mounted screen replacement,
battery replacement, charging-port sub-board swap, and connector disconnect/reconnect close-ups.
Re-film whenever you change practice-phone models. Keep raw files (no editing needed); a numbered
filename scheme matching this list (e.g. `video-4.1-own-iphone11-screen.mp4`) lets these
recordings gradually replace the YouTube entries above.
