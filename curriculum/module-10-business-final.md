# Module 10 — Repair Business Skills + Final Assessment

**Duration:** 2 h theory + 4 h exam
**Slides:** `slides/module-10-business-final.html`
**Practical:** `practicals/practical-10-final-exam.md`
**Videos:** see Module 10 section of `resources/videos.md`
**Images:** `resources/images/repair-workflow.svg`

## Learning objectives
By the end of this module, students can:
1. Run a repair through the full professional workflow: intake → diagnosis → quote &
   approval → repair → QC → return & warranty (`repair-workflow.svg`).
2. Price a repair with the formula (part cost + labor rate × hours + margin), apply a
   minimum charge, and explain the free-diagnosis trap.
3. Source parts by quality tier from vetted suppliers and keep basic inventory.
4. Handle customers professionally: set expectations, deliver bad news, resolve
   complaints and warranty claims.
5. Protect customer data and stay legal: privacy rules, stolen-device red flags, IMEI
   checks, activation-lock policy, and local e-waste rules.
6. Keep simple records and market a small repair shop honestly.

## Materials
- Printed blank intake forms, quote sheets, QC checklists, and warranty log pages
  (masters referenced in the slides — one set per student, they keep these).
- Sample supplier listings for one common screen at 3 quality tiers with prices, for
  the live pricing exercise.
- Role-play scenario cards (water-damage bad news, angry warranty return, "found
  phone" walk-in, lowball haggler).
- Final exam materials ready and sealed: `assessments/final-written-exam.md`,
  `assessments/final-practical-exam.md`, exam phones with planted faults per the
  practical exam sheet, graded-rubric folders per student.

## Session plan

### 1. Recap & quiz review (10 min) — slides 1–2
- 3 quick questions from Module 9 (backup-first, safe mode, activation-lock policy).
- Frame the day: "You can fix phones. Today is how you get *paid* to fix phones —
  then you prove all of it in the final exam."

### 2. The professional repair workflow (25 min) — slides 3–9
- Walk `repair-workflow.svg` end to end; every job, every time:
  1. **Intake form:** device + IMEI, fault description in the customer's words,
     **passcode handling policy** (needed for testing; stored sealed, deleted after),
     **photos of pre-existing damage** (your defense against "you scratched it"),
     signed terms (data risk, no-guarantee cases, abandoned-device clause).
  2. **Diagnosis** — and your **diagnosis fee policy**, stated up front.
  3. **Quote & approval** — no work before a yes; re-quote if you find more.
  4. **Repair** — screw mapping, new gaskets, all course discipline applies forever.
  5. **QC checklist** — full function test before the customer is called.
  6. **Return & warranty** — walk the customer through the test, state warranty
     terms in writing (e.g. 90 days on part + labor, never on water damage).
- ▶ **Video 10.1** (starting a phone repair business — see videos.md).

### 3. Pricing (20 min) — slides 10–14
- The formula: **price = part cost + (labor rate × hours) + margin.** Cost your labor
  honestly — including diagnosis, sourcing, and QC time, not just wrench time.
- **Minimum charge:** no job below it, even "quick" ones — port cleaning is 2 minutes
  of work and years of skill; charge for the skill (or strategically gift it — your
  choice, made consciously).
- **The free-diagnosis trap:** free diagnosis attracts tire-kickers and unpaid hours;
  common fix — a diagnosis fee credited against the repair if they proceed.
- Live exercise: class prices a screen repair from the sample supplier listings at
  all 3 quality tiers; compare answers and defend margins.
- ▶ **Video 10.2** (pricing & customer service for repair shops — see videos.md).

### 4. Parts, suppliers & inventory (15 min) — slides 15–18
- **Quality tiers** (from Module 4): original/service-pack, OEM-grade, aftermarket
  copy — sell the difference honestly with a price for each.
- **Supplier vetting:** samples first, small orders, check warranty/return terms
  (dead-on-arrival policy), delivery reliability > tiny price differences.
- Always order **spare screws, adhesive, gaskets** with every parts order — the $0.10
  part missing at reassembly delays the $80 job.
- **Inventory basics:** stock fast movers for your local market (top-5 models' screens,
  batteries, ports), order the rest per job; count monthly; log every part in/out.
- ▶ **Video 10.3** (parts sourcing & quality — see videos.md).

### 5. Customers, data & the law (25 min) — slides 19–25
- **Setting expectations:** repeat course scripts — water damage has no guarantee,
  serialized iPhone parts have caveats, opened phones lose water resistance.
- **Bad-news scripts:** water damage ("treatment fee, no promise, data first?") and
  board faults ("this needs a board-level specialist; here's the referral") —
  role-play both from the scenario cards.
- **Complaints & warranty claims:** listen fully, check the warranty log, fix fast
  and cheerfully when it's yours — a handled complaint creates a loyal customer.
- **Data privacy — never browse customer data.** Not photos, not messages, not once.
  Passcodes handled per your written policy; devices bought for resale get a
  documented full wipe.
- **Legal:** stolen-device red flags (no passcode known, price too eager, story that
  wobbles, "found it") → **check the IMEI** against blocklists; **activation lock =
  walk away** (Module 9's rule, now shop policy); dispose of batteries and boards per
  **local e-waste rules** — the battery bin has a legal life after the course too.

### 6. Records & marketing (10 min) — slides 26–28
- **Record keeping:** a job book (or spreadsheet) — date, customer, device, fault,
  work done, parts used, price, warranty end date. The warranty log settles disputes.
- **Marketing basics:** Google Business Profile with reviews, before/after photos
  (**with the customer's permission** — privacy rule applies to marketing too),
  fast turnaround as your headline selling point, honest quotes as your reputation.

### 7. Final exam briefing (15 min) — slides 29–31
- Structure of the finals (see `assessments/`):
  - **Written exam** — `assessments/final-written-exam.md` — covers all 10 modules;
    20% of the final grade.
  - **Practical exam** — run from `practicals/practical-10-final-exam.md` with
    `assessments/final-practical-exam.md` — real faulted phones, full workflow from
    intake form to QC checklist; 30% of the final grade and a hard pass requirement.
- Remind: quizzes (20%) and lab rubrics (30%) are already banked; safety violations
  fail the practical regardless of skill (trainer-guide rule).
- Questions, then a short break — exam conditions after.

### 8. FINAL EXAM (4 h)
Run `practicals/practical-10-final-exam.md` — this replaces the normal lab:
- Part 1: **final written exam** (from `assessments/final-written-exam.md`), closed
  book, ~1 h.
- Part 2: **final practical exam** (per `assessments/final-practical-exam.md`), ~3 h:
  each student receives a faulted phone and a "customer" (trainer/assistant), and
  must run the complete workflow — intake, diagnosis, quote, repair, QC, return —
  graded on the exam rubric.
- Collect all paperwork; grade against `assessments/certification-checklist.md` and
  issue certificates at the results session.

## Homework
- None — course complete. Graduates should: assemble their own toolkit (Module 1
  list), price the top-5 repairs for their local market using today's formula, and
  keep every course worksheet as their first shop manual.

## Common trainer pitfalls
- Don't let the business theory float free of the exam — the practical final grades
  the intake form and QC checklist too, so say so in the briefing.
- Students treat pricing as guesswork; force the formula in the live exercise and
  make them defend every number out loud.
- Don't skip the role-plays to save time — delivering bad news is a skill, and the
  first attempt should not be on a real customer.
- Verify every exam phone's planted fault the day before, and have one spare faulted
  phone ready — a dead exam phone under time pressure is unfair and unfixable.
- Keep exam conditions real: no coaching during the practical final. If you must
  intervene for safety, that station is re-run with a spare phone.
